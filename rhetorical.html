Rhetorical Analysis

Rather than documenting individual incidents in isolation, A Cartography of Genocide argues that repeated acts of violence become meaningful when understood as part of a broader spatial strategy. Through the aggregation of thousands of verified incidents into a single cartographic database, the project shifts attention away from singular events and towards recurring patterns of displacement, destruction, and territorial control. The central argument is therefore not simply that violence has occurred, but that its repetition across time and space demonstrates an organised and cumulative logic rather than a series of disconnected episodes.

The project is critical because it challenges conventional forms of evidence that often privilege written testimony or isolated visual documentation. Instead, Forensic Architecture positions cartography as a form of investigation capable of producing new evidence through spatial relationships. Maps are used not merely to represent geography, but to expose patterns that are difficult to perceive through individual reports alone. By combining open-source investigation with architectural and computational methods, the project reframes mapping as an active analytical practice capable of supporting legal accountability, public understanding, and human-rights investigations. In doing so, the work argues that cartography can function not only as a means of representation, but also as a political instrument for revealing systemic violence.
