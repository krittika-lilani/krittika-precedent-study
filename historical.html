Historical + Contextual Analysis

A Cartography of Genocide

A Cartography of Genocide emerged in 2024 during the ongoing Israeli genocide in Gaza following the attacks of 7 October 2023. By the time the project was published, the genocide had generated widespread destruction of civilian infrastructure, large-scale displacement, and an increasing number of allegations regarding violations of international humanitarian law. The investigation was developed within a rapidly evolving political and legal context in which governments, international organizations, journalists, and human rights groups were attempting to document events while access to Gaza remained severely restricted.

Rather than responding only to the destruction itself, Forensic Architecture responds to the problem of how violence can be documented when direct observation is limited or impossible. Traditional forms of evidence gathering, such as on-site forensic investigation, were often unavailable because of ongoing military operations, restrictions on access, and the scale of destruction. The project therefore turns to open-source intelligence, satellite imagery, geospatial analysis, witness testimony, and publicly available media as alternative forms of evidence. In this sense, the investigation reflects a broader shift in contemporary human rights research toward remote sensing and computational verification.

The project also reflects the evolution of Forensic Architecture as a research practice. Since its founding, the agency has developed methods that combine architecture, spatial analysis, computer vision, cartography, and investigative journalism to reconstruct events of political violence. A Cartography of Genocide extends this methodology from reconstructing individual incidents to identifying spatial and temporal patterns across an entire territory. Rather than asking whether one event occurred, the investigation examines whether repeated events reveal systematic forms of military conduct.

The historical context is also technological. The investigation depends on the widespread availability of commercial satellite imagery, open-source videos shared through social media platforms, GIS software, digital mapping tools, and computational methods capable of organizing large volumes of heterogeneous data. These technologies have transformed the production of evidence by allowing researchers to verify locations, reconstruct timelines, and analyze relationships across thousands of individual observations without requiring continuous physical access to the site itself.

Equally important is the project's legal context. The investigation was developed during ongoing proceedings before international legal institutions concerning allegations of genocide and violations of international humanitarian law. Rather than presenting itself as journalism or advocacy alone, the project is structured as an evidentiary resource that can contribute to legal processes. Its emphasis on verification, transparency of methodology, and spatial analysis reflects the standards required for evidence that may be examined within judicial settings.

The intended audience therefore extends beyond architects or designers. The project addresses international courts, human rights organizations, policymakers, journalists, researchers, and the wider public. Its interactive platform makes complex spatial evidence accessible to multiple audiences while maintaining a level of methodological transparency that allows others to evaluate, verify, and build upon the investigation.

Viewed within its historical context, A Cartography of Genocide demonstrates how computational design has expanded beyond the production of architectural form. The project positions computation as a means of documenting, organizing, and communicating spatial evidence in situations where conventional forms of investigation are constrained. Rather than producing architecture, it produces a framework through which contested events can be examined as spatial and legal questions.
