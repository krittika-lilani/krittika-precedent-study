Methodology

A Cartography of Genocide

Forensic Architecture combines architectural analysis, open-source investigation, remote sensing, and computational mapping to transform dispersed evidence into spatial analysis. Rather than collecting primary evidence in the field, the project aggregates videos, photographs, satellite imagery, witness testimony, historical maps, media reports, and datasets shared by trusted organisations. Each source is verified before being incorporated into the General Cartographic Database (GCD), an interactive spatial database that enables incidents to be mapped, filtered, and compared across both time and territory.

The report outlines a five-stage workflow consisting of collection, verification, interpretation, mapping, and analysis. Open-source investigation techniques—including geolocation, chronolocation, remote sensing, and cartographic regression—are used to authenticate individual incidents before they become part of a larger spatial dataset. Rather than focusing on isolated events, the GCD allows researchers to identify recurring patterns across thousands of verified incidents, revealing relationships that would be difficult to observe through conventional documentation alone. This methodology positions cartography not simply as a representational tool, but as an analytical framework through which spatial evidence is assembled, compared, and communicated for legal, public, and investigative contexts.
