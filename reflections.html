Reflections

A Cartography of Genocide

Wider Practice

A Cartography of Genocide builds directly upon Forensic Architecture's broader practice of investigating human-rights violations through spatial analysis, architectural reconstruction, and open-source intelligence. Across projects such as Saydnaya, The Murder of Halit Yozgat, and Cloud Studies, the agency consistently combines architectural knowledge with computational methods to reconstruct events that are difficult to document through conventional forms of evidence. Rather than treating architecture as the design of buildings alone, Forensic Architecture positions it as an investigative discipline capable of analysing space, violence, and state power.

Within this wider body of work, A Cartography of Genocide represents a shift in scale. Instead of reconstructing a single incident, the project analyses thousands of verified events through the General Cartographic Database to reveal long-term spatial patterns. While the methods remain consistent with the agency's investigative practice, the project extends them into a continuously evolving cartographic platform that functions simultaneously as a research archive, analytical tool, and public record.

Personal Assessment

I think one of the strongest aspects of this precedent is its ability to transform an overwhelming quantity of fragmented information into something that can be understood spatially. Rather than relying on a single piece of evidence, the project demonstrates how mapping can reveal relationships and recurring patterns that would otherwise remain difficult to identify. Its visual language is particularly effective given the gravity and complexity of the subject. The restrained use of colour, consistent cartographic conventions, minimal graphic treatment, and careful hierarchy prevent the investigation from becoming sensationalized while still communicating the scale of the violence. These visual decisions feel highly intentional: the maps remain analytical and legible, allowing the evidence to carry the emotional and political weight of the project.

At the same time, the project's scale can also become one of its limitations. The interactive platform and accompanying report contain an extraordinary amount of information, which can make it difficult for first-time viewers to identify the central argument without spending significant time exploring the database. While this complexity reflects the depth and rigour of the research, a clearer guided narrative or more explicit hierarchy could make the investigation more accessible to broader public audiences without simplifying its evidence.
