Ontological Analysis

A Cartography of Genocide

Unlike a conventional architectural project, A Cartography of Genocide does not produce a physical building or object. Instead, it constructs an evidentiary system through which spatial, temporal, visual, and legal information is assembled into a coherent investigation. The project's ontology is therefore composed not of architectural elements such as walls or rooms, but of interconnected datasets, computational processes, spatial relationships, and forms of evidence.

At its foundation are events. Rather than treating the destruction of buildings or infrastructure as isolated incidents, the investigation defines each event as a spatially and temporally located occurrence. Every recorded strike, evacuation order, destruction of civilian infrastructure, or displacement route exists as an individual unit that can be located, dated, verified, and connected to other events.

These events are supported by multiple forms of source material. The investigation combines satellite imagery, open-source videos, photographs, witness testimony, official military statements, humanitarian reports, and publicly available datasets. No single source is treated as sufficient evidence on its own. Instead, credibility emerges through the cross-referencing of multiple independent sources, allowing individual observations to become verified spatial evidence.

A second layer consists of computational processes that transform raw information into analyzable knowledge. Geolocation, image verification, temporal sequencing, GIS mapping, spatial analysis, and data classification are not simply research techniques; they function as active components of the project. These processes establish relationships between otherwise disconnected pieces of information, allowing patterns to emerge across both space and time.

The investigation also relies on a system of classification. Rather than presenting destruction as a collection of unrelated images, incidents are organized into categories such as attacks on hospitals, educational facilities, shelters, humanitarian infrastructure, agricultural land, evacuation zones, and areas designated as "safe." These categories provide the conceptual framework through which individual events become legible as part of a broader spatial pattern.

Another important ontological layer is the project's cartographic interface. The interactive map does not simply visualize data; it functions as the environment within which relationships between events are explored. Geographic coordinates, timelines, annotations, photographs, videos, and written descriptions are all linked through the map, making spatial relationships the primary organizational structure of the investigation.

Finally, the project produces knowledge claims rather than architectural form. The outputs are maps, timelines, analytical diagrams, legal reports, and interactive visualizations that collectively argue for identifiable patterns of military conduct. In this sense, the final object of the project is not the map itself, but a structured body of spatial evidence that can be examined by journalists, researchers, human rights organizations, policymakers, and international courts.

Viewed ontologically, A Cartography of Genocide demonstrates a shift in computational design from generating physical form toward constructing systems of knowledge. Its primary building blocks are not geometric objects, but events, datasets, classifications, spatial relationships, temporal sequences, and evidentiary links. Computation is therefore used not to design architecture, but to organize, verify, and communicate spatial evidence.
