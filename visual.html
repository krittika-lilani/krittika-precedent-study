Visual + Aesthetic Analysis

A Cartography of Genocide

Unlike many architectural projects, A Cartography of Genocide does not use visual representation to illustrate a design proposal. Instead, visual representation functions as the primary mechanism through which the investigation constructs and communicates evidence. Maps, satellite imagery, timelines, annotations, photographs, and diagrams do not simply accompany the argument; they constitute the argument itself.

The project's visual language is deliberately restrained. A limited color palette, clean typography, fine linework, and carefully layered information create an aesthetic that resembles scientific mapping and forensic documentation rather than architectural presentation. This restrained visual style reduces unnecessary decoration and directs attention toward relationships between spatial data, temporal sequences, and verified evidence. The absence of expressive graphics reinforces the project's claim to methodological transparency and analytical rigor.

One of the defining characteristics of the investigation is its use of layering. Satellite imagery is combined with geolocated photographs, annotations, witness testimony, timelines, and spatial boundaries within a single visual environment. Rather than presenting isolated images of destruction, the interface enables viewers to move between different forms of evidence and understand how individual observations contribute to larger spatial patterns. The visual system therefore encourages verification rather than passive observation.

The investigation also shifts architectural drawing away from its traditional role. Conventional architectural drawings often describe buildings before they exist, whereas the visualizations in A Cartography of Genocide reconstruct events that have already occurred. Cartography, spatial analysis, and computational visualization become investigative instruments rather than representational techniques. Drawing is transformed from a design tool into a method of producing knowledge.

The project's representational style situates it within an interdisciplinary community of practice that extends beyond architecture. Its visual language draws from geographic information systems, remote sensing, forensic science, investigative journalism, open-source intelligence, and legal evidence production. Rather than adopting the conventions of architectural rendering, the investigation aligns itself with disciplines in which visual credibility, traceability, and reproducibility are essential.

Viewed collectively, the project's visual language demonstrates that computational representation can operate as a form of argument. The interface does not ask viewers to accept a conclusion only through written explanation. Instead, it provides a visual framework through which patterns of destruction become observable, verifiable, and open to critical examination. In this project, representation is not the final stage of design; it is the method through which spatial knowledge is produced.
